"The man who never makes a mistake is the man who never does anything."

Theodore Roosevelt

"Can you make a dinosaur?"

The sun beat down over a line of 12 kids waiting for their turn. My apron sagged with the weight of over 100 balloons in varying colors, all waiting to be transformed into unicorns, ninjas, or monkeys. My feet ached after 3 hours of standing and my fingers, rubbed raw from constant tying and twisting, did not have the muscle memory to create a flawless dinosaur the same way they could fly to turn a shapeless tube into a cat, dog, or butterfly. But the 7 year old boy needed a dinosaur. And I needed to please every child in that line. Thinking quickly, my mouth ratteled off questions about the boy's favorite color, movie, candy, holiday, and I racked my brain for ways to create a shape that could possibly fit in with the Land Before Time characters. Before the boy could finish talking my fingers rallied to create what looked an awful lot like a giraffe - but when presented with a roar fulfilled a 7-year old's idea of what a dinosaur should look like.

I'll never be prepared for every scenario, but I have learned to think on my feet. Whether it's taking new instructions from my boss as I'm simultaneously trying to finish the task she gave me 10 minutes ago and answer a client's questions on the phone, learning how to make coffee for the guest lounge 15 minutes before the event starts, or turning a giraffe into a dinosaur, I'm not afraid to jump in and get the job done.
